Glass Melt Base Layer

How does music foster immersion? What I feel was successful, what challenges I had, what I need to know more about.

The base layer begins with bold looming chords in C-Major as the video unfolds. The chimes in the beginning are timed with the revealing of the glass in the chamber. The timing of the chimes make the glass appear to be somewhat magical. The accompaniment of C-Minor chords adds some melody to the track, but the changing volume levels help maintain and enhance the level of intensity.

When it’s time for the glass to finally melt, you begin to hear drumming which creates listener anticipation. The jungle drums in conjunction with the Sogawni presets result in an increased tempo, making the listener expectant of some activity. The glass upon finally falling, is accompanied by sad tones and some cymbals towards the end. This can be interpreted as leaving the listener wondering what happens next and how much sooner would more of the glass melt. The final tones of the track help signify the end of the video, like a “Game Over”.

The track fosters immersion because the arrangement utilizes varying sound levels and tempos to keep the listener’s attention. While the first half of the track may seem monotonous for a little longer than usual, it transitions to a faster tempo in just enough time that the listener does not lose interest.

I believe the track was successful especially in indicating that something was going to happen. The chimes in the beginning presented the glass, the drumming showed the glass falling over, and lastly, the tones at the end indicating that everything was over. I believe what challenged me with this assignment was auditioning and choosing potential sounds for the track. I believe with more experience I would be more able to detect what sounds I would use to create better tracks. I also believe that more practice would help me be bolder about being more unpredictable with my tracks.

Success in following Audio Design Document template provided:
The Audio Design Document was helpful in giving me a general idea of what kinds of sounds I would make, and also provided opportunity to organize them ahead of time. I was able to implement most of the sound I wanted, like the guillotine and traffic scenes.

How I followed and did not follow the design document:
I followed most of the document, but I made some adjustments. I have shortened the video because towards the end the scenes get much shorter and make it more difficult to follow through with the theme I had from the start. I also did not need any transition sounds. Initially the idea was to incorporate ticking sounds for each of the scenes to match the clock, but in the end I only used that with the default guess theme. The special scenes have their own music, so depending on the scene one can still sense of urgency. Most of the sounds effects for the death scenes were implemented as planned for the development document.

Cube Game - Midterm Project

My midterm project was to compose soundtracks and  sound effects to accompany a game developed using Cube. 

Deliverables would include a base layer track which would play throughout the game play, and would be layered over by level tracks (inside house, after red button press) and sound effects (heartbeat, health level) as users progress.

Students had the option of submitting video of walkthrough or performing a live demo, which I selected because it would make my midterm presentation more interactive.
